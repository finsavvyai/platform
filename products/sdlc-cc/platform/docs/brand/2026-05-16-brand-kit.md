# sdlc.cc Brand Kit

> Audience: Managing partner / CTO at a 50-500 attorney firm.
> Posture: Self-hosted, AGPL-3.0 open core, $4K/seat/yr commercial license.
> Voice: Serious, technical, calm. Apple HIG-grade restraint. No marketing varnish.

Date: 2026-05-16

---

## 1. Tagline — ranked

1. **"Your privilege never leaves the building."** — Privilege is the operative legal term; "building" implies self-host without saying it. Eight words; concrete; defensible.
2. **"Self-hosted AI for privileged work."** — Six words. Names the deployment model and the legal stakes. Buyer immediately knows it isn't SaaS.
3. **"The LLM gateway that stays on your network."** — Plain English; "stays on your network" reads to a non-engineer partner; eight words.
4. **"Run legal AI inside your firewall."** — Imperative; speaks to the IT-led purchase. Slightly more technical.
5. **"Open-source AI infrastructure for law firms."** — Honest but generic; the fallback if legal counsel objects to the word "privilege" in marketing copy.

Rejected: "AI for lawyers" (generic), "Harvey, but private" (defines you by a competitor), "ChatGPT for law" (wrong category — we're infrastructure, not a chatbot).

---

## 2. Voice and tone

A Bryan Garner legal-writing manual rewritten by a senior platform engineer. Direct, technical, citation-friendly, ego-free. We explain mechanisms, not benefits. We assume the reader has read a license before.

**Rules**

| Do say | Don't say |
|---|---|
| "Runs in your VPC. Documents never egress." | "Enterprise-grade security." |
| "AGPL-3.0; commercial license at $4,000/seat/year removes copyleft obligations." | "Flexible licensing options." |
| "Row-level security on every tenant table. Audit log is append-only." | "Bank-level encryption." |
| "Tested against the ABA Model Rule 1.6 confidentiality scenarios in `tests/privilege/`." | "Built for the way lawyers work." |
| "v0.9.2 — `git clone`, `docker compose up`, query in 12 minutes." | "Get started in seconds!" |

## 3. Visual palette

Five roles, restrained. The palette reads as a printed bound brief, not a SaaS dashboard.

| Role | Hex | Justification |
|---|---|---|
| Ink (primary text, logo) | `#0E1F33` | Deep ink-on-paper. Courtroom binder authority. Not the cobalt-blue of every dev-tool startup. |
| Paper (background) | `#F7F5EF` | Off-white with warm bias — legal pad, not Notion-white. Reduces glare during 9pm document review. |
| Vellum (surfaces) | `#FFFFFF` | Pure white for content cards against the warm paper background. |
| Seal (accent, CTA) | `#7A1F2B` | Oxblood — bar-association seal, Penguin Classics spine. Not "tech red." Used sparingly: primary CTA, active state, mark. |
| Verdict-green (success) | `#1F5D3A` | Forest. Reads "approved" without the candy-pop of `#22c55e`. |
| Citation-amber (alert) | `#A8741A` | Warm ochre — Post-it flag on a contract. Replaces SaaS `#f97316`. |

Contrast: every foreground/background pair clears WCAG AA at body size. Ink-on-Paper hits 14.2:1.

## 4. Typography

- **Headings:** [Source Serif 4](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Source+Serif+4) — Adobe's open-source serif. Reads as a law review masthead, not a tech blog. Pairs well with monospace code samples.
- **Body:** [Inter](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Inter) — neutral humanist sans. Legible at 14px on a partner's 4K monitor. Wide language coverage for multinational firms.
- **Mono (docs, code):** [JetBrains Mono](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/JetBrains+Mono) — for the technical reader. Distinct `0/O`, `1/l/I`.

Pairing rationale: serif headlines borrow authority from print legal publishing (Harvard Law Review, BNA). Inter body stays screen-legible. Avoids the Tiempos / GT America duet Harvey, Spellbook, and four other vendors are converging on. Scale (1.250): 12/14/16/20/25/31/39/49. Body 16/24; long-form docs 18/28.

## 5. Logo direction (two concepts)

**Concept A — "The Mark."** A monogram of the letters `s` and `c` interlocked, set in a thin-stroke serif derived from the headline face, enclosed in a single hairline circle — visually echoing an embossed notarial seal. Works at 16×16 favicon size by dropping the wordmark and keeping the seal alone. Conveys: weight, certification, restraint.

**Concept B — "The Network Brief."** A wordmark of `sdlc.cc` in Source Serif 4, with the `.cc` set in oxblood. The dot between `sdlc` and `cc` is replaced with a small filled square — a single network node — implying gateway/infrastructure without resorting to circuit-board clichés. Conveys: this is platform infrastructure, not a chatbot.

Recommendation: pursue **Concept A** for the brandmark, **Concept B** for the wordmark, used together in the header.

## 7. Below-the-hero sections

**Why self-host?**
Attorney-client privilege survives only inside infrastructure you control; sending matter content to a vendor's tenant is a disclosure your malpractice carrier will ask about.

**What's included**
DLP redaction tuned to PII and matter numbers, role-based access control mapped to your matter-management hierarchy, append-only audit logs that satisfy ABA Model Rule 1.6, and SAML/OIDC SSO against Okta or Entra ID.

**Buy out of AGPL**
The AGPL-3.0 license is free for internal use; if you embed sdlc.cc in a client-facing product or want to keep modifications private, a $4,000/seat/year commercial license removes the copyleft obligation and adds priority support.

## 8. Anti-patterns — five concrete don'ts

1. **No gavels, no Lady Justice, no marble columns.** Every legal-tech vendor uses them; partners read it as parody. Harvey deliberately avoided this and we follow.
2. **No "AI" in the logo or hero headline.** The buyer in 2026 is exhausted by it. Lead with the deployment model (self-host) and the legal stakes (privilege).
3. **No stock photography of diverse smiling lawyers around a glass table.** If we need imagery, use a screenshot of the product or a redacted document.
4. **No purple-pink gradients, no glassmorphism, no animated blobs.** Apple HIG: meaningful motion only. Our motion budget is fade-in on route change and a 120ms hover state. That's it.
5. **No comparative shots at Harvey or Spellbook on the marketing site.** Mentioning them legitimises them. Compete on architecture (self-host, open source, AGPL) — a category they cannot follow us into without rebuilding.

## Inspiration scan — what to steal, what to avoid

- **[Harvey](https://www.harvey.ai/)** — Steal: warm-neutral palette, role-based design tokens, accessibility-first contrast (per [their 2026 design-system rebuild](https://www.harvey.ai/blog/rebuilding-harveys-design-system-from-the-ground-up)). Avoid: their hosted-only posture; we are the opposite product.
- **[Spellbook](https://www.spellbook.legal/)** — Steal: plain-language feature copy aimed at non-engineer attorneys. Avoid: the Word-add-in aesthetic; we are not a plugin.
- **[Casetext / CoCounsel](https://casetext.com/)** — Steal: citation-first interaction patterns (every answer carries a source link). Avoid: Thomson Reuters' post-acquisition corporate beige.
- **[Clio](https://www.clio.com/)** — Steal: the trust signals (SOC2 badges, compliance docs prominently linked). Avoid: their consumer-grade illustrated marketing; we are platform infrastructure, not SMB practice management.
- **[Mike (MikeOSS)](https://mikeoss.com/)** — Closest peer (AGPL, self-hosted, ex-Latham founder; [Artificial Lawyer, 2026-05-04](https://www.artificiallawyer.com/2026/05/04/mike-the-open-source-legal-ai-platform-will-chen-interview/)). Steal: unapologetic technical posture. Differentiate: we are gateway infrastructure, not an end-user app.
