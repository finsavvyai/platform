I looked at Wiz, Snyk, Linear, Vercel, Resend, Supabase, Cal.com, and PostHog. Then I evaluated Bolt.new, v0.dev, and Lovable as rebuild tools. Here's the honest, brutal comparison and my recommendation.

## PART 2: THE HARSH VERDICT ON YOUR CURRENT LANDING PAGE

Here is the blunt truth: **Your landing page is a B+ student who studied all the templates but has no original voice.**

It follows the playbook correctly — hero with problem statement, fear section, solution section, comparison table, CTA. But it follows it so by-the-book that it feels generated, not crafted. And in many places, it follows the playbook from 2022 (text-heavy, dark mode, feature bullets) rather than the 2025–2026 playbook (visual-first, interactive, proof-driven).

The specific failures are these. First, **no visual content at all on the homepage** — in 2026 this is unforgivable. Every successful competitor shows the product. You show text about the product. Second, **the "fear" section is too long and too aggressive.** Four cards describing how agents can destroy you, mocking self-hosting with sarcasm in the comparison table — this reads like FUD marketing from a 2015 antivirus company. Modern security companies (Wiz, Snyk) lead with empowerment, not fear. Third, **the page is too long.** I count 9 sections across 8+ scroll depths. By section 5 (the comparison table), user attention is gone. Linear's homepage is half the length and twice as effective. Fourth, **the "73% of self-hosted AI agents have critical security misconfigurations" stat in the hero badge** — where does this come from? There's no citation, no source link. If you made it up, it's a liability. If it's real, cite it. Fifth, **the TokenForge code snippet is clever but confusing.** Non-technical visitors (CISOs, GRC analysts) won't understand ECDSA P-256 keypairs. Technical visitors will wonder if this is real code or a mockup. It falls into a valley between "too technical to be marketing" and "too marketing to be technical." Sixth, **the stats counter section (Security Layers: 0, Score Categories: 0)** appears to use scroll-triggered animations that may not fire correctly. When I saw it, it showed low numbers mid-animation. If the counters fail, the section actively shows "0" for everything — making you look like you have zero of everything.

## PART 3: SHOULD YOU REBUILD WITH AN AI TOOL?

### The Tool Options

**Bolt.new** — Full-stack web app builder. Generates React/Next.js/Vite apps from prompts. Good for rapid prototyping entire applications. Can import from Figma. Best for building functional apps quickly, less ideal for pixel-perfect landing pages.

**v0.dev (by Vercel)** — Generates individual UI components and pages using shadcn/ui + Tailwind. Outputs clean React/Next.js code. Has landing page templates. Best for generating specific sections or components that you then assemble.

**Lovable** — Similar to Bolt.new, full-stack app generation from prompts. Good for quick MVPs. Can deploy instantly.

### My Recommendation: Use v0.dev for the Landing Page Rebuild

Here's why, and here's exactly how to do it.

**Don't rebuild the entire product.** Your dashboard, docs, pricing page, and demo are functional. The backend works. The problem is specifically the public-facing landing page and the brand presentation layer.

**Use v0.dev to generate a new landing page** because it outputs shadcn/ui + Tailwind (which you're already using), it produces clean Next.js code you can drop into your existing project, it's made by Vercel so the code quality matches your stack, and it has landing page templates as starting points.

**Don't use Bolt.new for this** because it will generate an entirely separate app that you'd have to integrate back into your Next.js project. That's wasted effort. Bolt.new is better when you're building something from scratch.

**Don't use Lovable for this** for the same reason — it creates standalone apps, and you need components that fit into your existing Next.js + Tailwind project.

### The Rebuild Strategy

Here's exactly what I'd do in a weekend:

**Step 1: Design the new landing page in v0.dev.**

Give v0.dev this prompt (or something like it):

"Build a dark-mode landing page for an AI agent security platform called OpenSyber. Style: similar to Linear.app and Supabase.com. Hero section with a short 4–6 word headline, a subheading, two CTAs (primary blue and secondary outline), and an embedded product screenshot/mockup showing a security dashboard with a score of 82/100, severity badges, and health metrics. Below the hero: a scrolling logo bar for trust signals. Then a section showing the product in three panels (Infrastructure, Marketplace, Monitoring) with actual UI screenshots. Then a comparison table (us vs self-hosting). Then a final CTA. Use Tailwind, shadcn/ui, dark background (#0a0a0a), blue-600 primary, green for positive indicators, subtle neutral-800 borders. Make it feel premium and technical, not enterprise-boring."

v0 will generate the entire page structure. You'll iterate 3–5 times to get it right.

**Step 2: Take screenshots of your actual dashboard and embed them.**

Go to your demo page and the real dashboard. Take clean screenshots of: the security score donut chart with the score breakdown, the event feed with HIGH/MEDIUM/INFO severity badges, the health metrics (CPU/Memory/Disk), and the achievements page. Clean up any test data. These screenshots become the visual backbone of your new landing page.

**Step 3: Replace the current homepage.**

Take the v0-generated React components, drop them into your `app/page.tsx`, and replace the existing homepage. Keep your current nav, footer, and routing intact. You're only replacing the marketing content between them.

**Step 4: Fix the critical issues from the previous audit simultaneously.**

While rebuilding the landing page, also fix: security headers (add them in `next.config.js`), the OG image (create it and upload it), the Clerk production mode (swap the key), the 404 page (create a branded `not-found.tsx`), and add a sitemap.

This entire rebuild is a weekend project, not a month-long redesign.

## PART 4: THE NEW LANDING PAGE BLUEPRINT

Based on everything I've learned from competitors, here is exactly what the new OpenSyber landing page should look like, section by section:

**Section 1: Hero (above the fold)**

Left side: "Security for AI agents." (headline, 4 words, massive). Below: one sentence — "Deploy on hardened infrastructure. Monitor every action. Stop threats in real time." Two CTAs: "Start Free →" (blue-600, solid) and "See Live Demo →" (white outline, border). Below CTAs: "Free forever. No credit card. Deploy in 60 seconds."

Right side: A polished, slightly angled screenshot of the security dashboard showing the security score donut at 82, the running status, and 2–3 recent events. Add a subtle glow/gradient behind it. This screenshot does more selling than any copy.

**Section 2: Trust Bar**

A single-line scrolling logo bar. If you don't have customers yet, show: "Open source on GitHub" with a star count, "Deployed on AWS/Cloudflare" badges, "SOC2 in progress" badge, "GDPR compliant" badge. Even framework and tool logos (Next.js, Docker, Tailwind) used to build the product add trust at this stage.

**Section 3: The Problem (condensed)**

Not four separate cards. One single, concise section: "AI agents run code, access credentials, and talk to the internet. Without OpenSyber, you have no visibility, no control, and no audit trail." Pair this with a split visual: left side shows a dark, scary terminal with unsecured agent output; right side shows the OpenSyber dashboard with a green 92/100 score. Before/after. Visual. Instant understanding.

**Section 4: Three Pillars (with screenshots)**

Three columns, each with a REAL screenshot: "Hardened Infrastructure" with a screenshot of the deployment/settings page showing the region, encrypted vault, firewall status. "Verified Marketplace" with a screenshot of the skill marketplace (you need to seed it with 5+ first-party skills first). "Security Dashboard" with a screenshot of the score breakdown and event feed.

Each column: icon, heading, one sentence, screenshot, and a "Learn more →" link to docs.

**Section 5: Interactive Demo Embed**

Instead of a static comparison table, embed an interactive version of your `/demo` page directly on the homepage. Let visitors click through Overview/Events/Network tabs, see the score breakdown, and explore events. Make it feel like they're already using the product. Add a subtle overlay: "This is a live demo. Sign up to monitor your own agents."

**Section 6: How It Works (simplified)**

Three steps, horizontal: "Sign Up → Deploy → Monitor." Each step: number, icon, one sentence. That's it. No paragraphs. The simplicity IS the message.

**Section 7: Social Proof & Trust**

If you have zero customers, use these alternatives: a quote from someone using the open-source version, a GitHub contributor count, a number of deployments or agents monitored (even from your own testing), any press mention or Hacker News comment. If truly nothing: "Join 200+ developers on the waitlist" (if you have a waitlist) or "Backed by [accelerator]" (if applicable).

**Section 8: Final CTA**

"Stop running AI agents blind." One sentence. One button: "Start Free →". Clean. Done.

That's 8 sections versus your current 9+. Each section has a visual element. The total page length is roughly 60% of your current page. Every section either shows the product, proves trust, or converts.

## PART 5: THE BRAND QUESTION

The deeper problem isn't the landing page layout — it's that OpenSyber doesn't have a brand yet. It has a color scheme and a name. That's not a brand.

Wiz has the magnet icon, the blue, and the approachable illustration style. Snyk has the dog mascot and the purple-to-dark gradient. Linear has the minimalist precision and the fanned-card logo. Supabase has the green electricity bolt and the developer community. PostHog has the hedgehog and the anti-corporate personality. Cal.com has the white-space-and-warmth aesthetic.

OpenSyber has... a blue shield and a dark background. That describes 200 other security companies.

You need to decide: What is OpenSyber's visual personality? Is it the "control room" — dark, dense, real-time data, operator vibes (think Bloomberg Terminal meets security)? Is it the "guardian" — protective, warm despite being dark, with subtle green-for-safe signals everywhere? Is it the "hacker's ally" — terminal-native, monospace-heavy, raw and authentic, showing real code and real commands? Is it the "enterprise shield" — polished, corporate-friendly, plenty of whitespace, certifications front and center?

Pick one. Let it infect everything — the landing page, the dashboard, the docs, the blog, the social media, the email templates, the error pages. Right now you're a bit of each and therefore none of them.

My recommendation: go with the "control room" identity. It plays to your demo dashboard's strengths (which genuinely looks like a security operations center), it differentiates you from Wiz/Snyk (who are going corporate/enterprise), and it appeals to your primary persona (the technical builder who wants to feel like they have real-time command over their agents).
