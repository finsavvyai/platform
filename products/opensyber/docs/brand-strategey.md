This is a brand strategy problem masquerading as a design question. Let me build the full "Control Room" identity system — color palette, typography, voice, UI components, and usage rules — as a living brand guide.The full brand identity system is live. Here's what it commits to:

**The identity: "The Operator."** Not a guardian, not a hacker, not a corporate shield — the person in the control room who sees everything in real time and acts with precision when it matters.

**The key decisions made, and why they're irreversible:**

The **signal colour is electric teal (#00E5C3)**, not another blue. Blue is what 200 security companies use. Teal reads as "live system active" — it's the colour of a healthy radar return, a heartbeat monitor at rest, a terminal cursor blinking. It earns its appearance.

**Bebas Neue** for display text is the identity at scale — compressed, authoritative, no-nonsense. It's the difference between "we have a typeface" and "you recognise us in one second at a conference."

**Space Mono everywhere in the UI** means the product literally looks like a terminal that got a design system. That's the point. The competitors are going the other direction — more illustration, more warmth, more enterprise polish. OpenSyber goes further into the machine.

**The voice rules are the hardest to enforce** — no exclamation marks inside the product, no "Oops!" error messages, real timestamps on everything, specific names always. The operator doesn't need comfort, they need the failure mode.

The brand guide covers: identity rationale, full colour palette, type system with specimens, three logo mark concepts (crosshair is recommended), live UI components, voice and tone with do/don't pairs, motion principles with working animations, and non-negotiable usage rules across every surface.
